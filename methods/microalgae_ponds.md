# Microalgae Ponds (Land-Based) for Carbon Capture

## Key Player: Brilliant Planet (UK / Morocco)

**Method Summary:** Microalgae are cultivated in large, shallow open-air saltwater ponds constructed on barren coastal desert land. The algae photosynthetically absorb CO2 from the atmosphere as they grow, converting it into biomass. Once harvested, the algae biomass is solar-dried and buried in the hyper-arid desert soil, where the absence of moisture prevents decomposition — locking the carbon away for thousands of years. The "Desert Sponge" efficiency hack leverages cheap, otherwise-useless desert land (Morocco's Atlantic Saharan coast), gravity-fed seawater flow from nearby coastline, and intense year-round solar irradiance to minimize costs. Unlike open-ocean algae farming, land-based ponds are fully controlled, avoiding storm damage, ecological disruption, and measurement uncertainty.

## 1. Technology Overview

### 1.1 How It Works

Brilliant Planet's process follows a multi-stage biological carbon capture pipeline:

1. **Seawater intake:** Seawater is pumped (or gravity-fed where coastal topography allows) into a series of progressively larger open raceway ponds on flat desert terrain. Ocean water naturally contains dissolved nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, trace metals) and a seed population of local microalgae species.
2. **Algae cultivation:** In shallow ponds (~15-30 cm deep), native microalgae species bloom rapidly under intense desert sunlight. Paddlewheels or other low-energy mixing systems circulate the water to keep algae suspended and ensure even light exposure. The algae photosynthesize, absorbing atmospheric CO2 (and dissolved CO2 from seawater) to build biomass.
3. **Harvesting:** Once the algae culture reaches target density, the biomass is separated from the water using flocculation (often pH- or bio-flocculant-induced settling) and/or simple filtration. The remaining brine/water is either recirculated or returned to the ocean.
4. **Solar drying:** The harvested wet algae paste is spread on drying beds and dehydrated using ambient desert heat and low humidity — no fossil energy required for drying. The desert climate (high temperatures, low humidity, constant wind) is ideal for rapid evaporative drying.
5. **Burial / sequestration:** The dried algae biomass is buried in the hyper-arid desert subsurface, where extremely low moisture conditions (<50 mm annual rainfall) prevent microbial decomposition. The carbon in the buried biomass is effectively locked away for millennia.

### 1.2 Algae Biology

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Organisms** | Native marine microalgae species (naturally occurring in local seawater); not genetically modified |
| **Dominant species** | Varies by location and season; often diatoms, chlorophytes, or other fast-growing marine species |
| **Growth medium** | Unmodified or minimally supplemented natural seawater |
| **Carbon fixation mechanism** | Photosynthesis — 6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6O2 |
| **Carbon content of dry biomass** | ~45-55% carbon by weight |
| **CO2 equivalence** | ~1.65-1.83 kg CO2 sequestered per kg dry algae biomass (stoichiometric ratio) |
| **Growth rate** | Doubling time of 1-3 days under optimal conditions |
| **Photosynthetic efficiency** | ~3-8% of incident solar energy converted to biomass (far higher than terrestrial crops at ~0.5-2%) |

### 1.3 The "Desert Sponge" Advantage

Brilliant Planet's core insight is that coastal deserts offer a unique combination of free resources for algae carbon capture:

| Resource | Desert Advantage | Economic Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Land** | Barren, non-arable desert land with near-zero alternative use | Land costs ~$0-50/ha vs. $5,000-50,000/ha for agricultural land |
| **Sunlight** | 2,500-3,500+ hours/year of intense solar irradiance; year-round growing season | No artificial lighting; continuous high productivity |
| **Seawater** | Adjacent to coastline; gravity-fed flow possible in many locations | Minimal pumping energy; free nutrient supply |
| **Heat for drying** | Ambient desert temperatures 30-50°C; humidity <20% | Solar drying replaces energy-intensive thermal drying |
| **Storm protection** | Land-based ponds are not subject to ocean storms, waves, or currents | Unlike open-ocean seaweed farming — no crop loss from storms |
| **No freshwater needed** | Entire process uses seawater; zero freshwater consumption | No competition with agriculture or drinking water |
| **No ecosystem displacement** | Barren desert has minimal existing biodiversity to disrupt | No deforestation or habitat destruction concerns |

### 1.4 Comparison: Land-Based Algae Ponds vs. Other Biological CDR

| Parameter | Microalgae Ponds (Brilliant Planet) | Reforestation | Open-Ocean Seaweed Farming | Biochar |

### 2.1 Land / Surface Footprint

| **For 1 Mt CO2/yr** | ~12,500-33,000 ha (125-330 km²) | Derived; approximately the size of a small city |
| **For 1 Gt CO2/yr** | ~12.5-33 million ha (125,000-330,000 km²) | Derived; roughly the area of Germany to Poland |
| **For 10 Gt CO2/yr** | ~125-330 million ha (1.25-3.3 million km²) | Derived; roughly the area of Peru to India |

**Key notes on land:**
- The land required is specifically **coastal desert** — it must be near the ocean (for seawater supply) and in a hyper-arid zone (for solar drying and stable burial).
- Not all desert is suitable: the site must have relatively flat terrain, proximity to coastline (within ~5-20 km for practical pumping/piping), and stable geology for burial.
- The footprint includes ponds, evaporation/drying areas, access roads, and processing areas. Burial sites may be co-located or nearby.

### 2.3 Location Dependency

| Factor | Requirement | Impact of Suboptimal Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Solar irradiance** | >2,000 kWh/m²/yr (ideally >2,500) | Lower growth rates; reduced productivity per hectare |
| **Annual rainfall** | <100 mm/yr (ideally <50 mm) | Higher rainfall risks pond dilution, burial instability, and unwanted decomposition of buried biomass |
| **Proximity to coast** | <5-20 km (for practical seawater delivery) | Greater distance increases pumping energy (OPEX) and pipeline costs (CAPEX) |
| **Coastal topography** | Ideally sloping down from ocean to site (gravity feed) | If site is above sea level, pumping energy is significant (~2.7 kWh per 1,000 m³ per 10m lift) |
| **Seawater nutrient content** | Upwelling zones preferred (naturally nutrient-rich) | Non-upwelling water may require nutrient supplementation, increasing costs |
| **Terrain flatness** | Flat or gently sloping land for pond construction | Hilly terrain requires more earthworks, increasing CAPEX |
| **Geological stability** | Stable geology for burial permanence | Seismic or flood-prone areas risk carbon release |
| **Political stability** | Essential for long-term infrastructure investment | Regions with conflict or unstable governance are impractical |

## 3. Resource Constraints

### 3.1 Energy Requirements

| Process Step | Energy Demand | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Seawater pumping (intake)** | ~0.5-5 kWh/t CO2 (highly site-dependent) | Electricity (solar PV ideal) | Gravity-fed sites near zero; 10-20m lift requires significant pumping |
| **Pond mixing (paddlewheels)** | ~2-10 kWh/t CO2 | Electricity | Low-speed paddlewheels; continuous operation |
| **Harvesting (flocculation/filtration)** | ~1-5 kWh/t CO2 | Electricity | Chemical flocculants or bioflocculants; simple filtration |
| **Drying** | ~0 kWh/t CO2 (thermal) | Ambient solar heat | Key advantage: desert sun provides free drying energy |
| **Burial (earthmoving)** | ~1-5 kWh/t CO2 | Diesel or electric equipment | Excavation, transport, and compaction of dried biomass |
| **Monitoring and controls** | ~0.5-2 kWh/t CO2 | Electricity | Sensors, data systems, site management |
| **Total energy per ton CO2** | **~5-30 kWh/t CO2** | Mixed (mostly electricity) | ~10-100x less energy-intensive than mechanical/chemical DAC |

**Key insight:** The energy advantage of biological carbon capture is enormous. Solid sorbent DAC requires ~1,500-2,500 kWh/t CO2 (thermal + electrical). Microalgae ponds require roughly ~5-30 kWh/t CO2 because the sun provides the energy for the actual CO2 capture (photosynthesis) and for drying. The main energy costs are just moving water and mixing ponds.

**Energy source:** Brilliant Planet plans to power pumping and mixing with on-site solar PV, making the entire process nearly zero-carbon from an energy standpoint in desert locations with abundant solar resource.

### 3.2 Water Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Freshwater consumption** | **Zero** | Entire process uses seawater; no freshwater inputs required |
| **Seawater intake volume** | ~50,000-200,000 m³/ha/yr (estimated) | Continuous flow-through to maintain salinity and nutrient supply |
| **Evaporative losses** | High — ~2,000-3,500 mm/yr in desert climates | Net evaporation from open ponds; increases salinity over time |
| **Salinity management** | Continuous seawater inflow; brine discharge or solar salt production | Must prevent hyper-salinification that kills algae (>50-60 ppt lethal for most species) |
| **Brine discharge** | Concentrated brine returned to ocean or evaporation ponds | Potential environmental concern — see Section 7.7 |
| **Water recycling** | Partial — post-harvest water can be recirculated before discharge | Reduces total seawater intake volume |

**No freshwater competition:** This is a major advantage over terrestrial bioenergy, reforestation, or BECCS, all of which compete for freshwater resources.

### 3.3 Materials and Infrastructure

| Component | Material Requirements | Lifecycle | Cost Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pond construction** | Earthworks (grading, compaction); pond liners (HDPE/LDPE or clay compaction) or unlined (compacted desert soil) | 15-30 years for HDPE liners; indefinite for earthworks | Major CAPEX component |
| **Pond liners** | HDPE geomembrane (~1.0-1.5 mm thick) or compacted native soil (if impermeability sufficient) | HDPE: 15-30 year lifetime | $2-8/m² for HDPE; much less if unlined |
| **Paddlewheel mixers** | Steel, fiberglass, or composite; electric motors | 10-20 years with maintenance | Moderate cost |
| **Seawater piping/channels** | HDPE pipe, concrete channels, or lined earthen channels | 20-50 years | Significant for long-distance conveyance |
| **Pumps** | Standard industrial seawater pumps (corrosion-resistant) | 10-15 years | Moderate CAPEX; OPEX for electricity |
| **Harvesting equipment** | Settling tanks, flocculant dosing systems, belt filters or centrifuges | 10-20 years | Moderate |
| **Drying beds** | Flat paved or lined areas; possibly greenhouse-type covers to accelerate drying | 15-30 years | Low cost (passive infrastructure) |
| **Nutrients** | Primarily from natural seawater; possible supplementation with agricultural-grade nitrogen/phosphorus | Ongoing OPEX | Low if seawater nutrients are sufficient; moderate if supplementation needed |
| **Flocculants** | Chemical (ferric chloride, alum) or bio-flocculants (chitosan, microbial) | Ongoing OPEX consumable | Low-moderate cost per ton biomass |
| **Burial infrastructure** | Earthmoving equipment; possible encapsulation material for long-term stability | Equipment: 10-20 years | Moderate |
| **Monitoring/MRV systems** | Sensors (pH, DO, chlorophyll, salinity); weather stations; drone/satellite imagery | Ongoing | Low-moderate |

**Nutrient sourcing — a critical question:**
- Brilliant Planet emphasizes using naturally nutrient-rich seawater from coastal upwelling zones (Morocco's Atlantic coast benefits from the Canary Current upwelling).
- In non-upwelling areas, supplemental nutrients (primarily nitrogen and phosphorus) may be needed, adding cost and raising questions about lifecycle emissions from fertilizer production.
- At gigaton scale, nutrient supply could become a bottleneck if natural ocean nutrients are insufficient.

| Nutrient | Approximate Requirement per t CO2 | Global Supply Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Nitrogen (as N)** | ~15-30 kg N/t CO2 (Redfield ratio-based estimate) | Global nitrogen fertilizer production: ~150 Mt N/yr |
| **Phosphorus (as P)** | ~2-4 kg P/t CO2 | Global phosphate rock production: ~220 Mt/yr (~30 Mt P/yr) |
| **For 1 Gt CO2/yr** | ~15-30 Mt N + ~2-4 Mt P | 10-20% of global N production; 7-13% of global P production |

If natural seawater nutrients are insufficient at scale, the nutrient demand could represent a significant fraction of global fertilizer production — a potential constraint.

### 4.3 Seasonal and Climate Factors

| Factor | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Year-round operation in desert** | **Yes** — no winter shutdown in subtropical/tropical deserts | Morocco (27-30°N latitude) has adequate irradiance year-round |
| **Diurnal cycle** | Photosynthesis only during daylight (~10-14 hours); at night, respiration consumes ~5-15% of daytime carbon fixation | Night respiration is a small loss; net positive over 24h cycle |
| **Cloud cover** | Minimal in desert locations (<10% annual cloud fraction) | Saharan coast has ~3,000+ sunshine hours/yr |
| **Temperature extremes** | Desert daytime temperatures can exceed 45°C; some algae species become stressed >35-40°C | Species selection for thermotolerance; pond depth management; evaporative cooling effect on water |
| **Wind (sand/dust)** | Saharan dust storms can reduce light and contaminate ponds | Pond design to minimize sand ingress; settling ponds |
| **Evaporation rate** | ~2,000-3,500 mm/yr from open ponds in desert | Managed by continuous seawater inflow; salinity monitoring |

## 5. End Game: Storage

### 5.1 Burial in Hyper-Arid Desert

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| **Storage method** | Dried algae biomass buried in shallow trenches or pits in desert soil |
| **Burial depth** | ~1-5 meters below surface (sufficient to prevent wind erosion and UV degradation) |
| **Burial form** | Compressed/dried algae bricks, pellets, or loose dried biomass |
| **Moisture content at burial** | <10% (solar-dried) |
| **Carbon content** | ~45-55% of dry mass = ~0.5-0.6 t C per t dry biomass = ~1.65-2.0 t CO2 equivalent per t dry biomass |

### 5.2 Permanence and Stability

| Factor | Assessment | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Decomposition risk** | Very low in hyper-arid conditions | Microbial decomposition requires moisture; at <50 mm/yr rainfall, microbial activity is extremely limited |
| **Natural analogue** | Organic material preserved for thousands of years in deserts | Egyptian mummies, Dead Sea Scrolls, dried plant material in Atacama (millions of years old in some cases) |
| **Estimated permanence** | 1,000-10,000+ years | Dependent on continued aridity; climate change could alter precipitation patterns |
| **Climate change risk** | Low-Moderate | Saharan/Arabian/Atacama deserts are predicted to remain hyper-arid under most climate scenarios; some edge regions may see increased rainfall |
| **Fire risk** | Very low | Buried biomass has minimal oxygen exposure; surface desert has little combustible material |
| **Flood risk** | Very low in properly sited locations | Flash floods are rare in hyper-arid regions; site selection avoids wadis/dry riverbeds |
| **Geological stability** | Generally high | Most target desert regions are tectonically stable |
| **Reversal mechanism** | Would require excavation or sustained moistening of buried biomass | Deliberate reversal unlikely; natural reversal very slow even if conditions change |

### 5.3 Comparison of Storage Permanence

| Storage Method | Permanence | Reversal Risk | Verification Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Desert burial (dried algae)** | 1,000-10,000+ years | Very low (if arid conditions persist) | Low-Moderate (can physically inspect burial sites) |
| **Geological injection (CCS)** | 10,000+ years | Very low (deep underground) | Moderate (subsurface monitoring) |
| **Basalt mineralization (Carbfix)** | Effectively permanent | Near-zero (solid rock) | Low (mineralogy confirmation) |
| **Reforestation** | 50-100 years | High (fire, disease, logging) | Moderate (satellite monitoring) |
| **Ocean biomass sinking** | Uncertain (100-1,000+ years?) | Uncertain (ocean circulation, decomposition at depth) | Very high (deep ocean monitoring) |
| **Biochar in soil** | 100-1,000+ years | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| **Enhanced weathering** | 10,000+ years | Near-zero | High (distributed, slow process) |

### 5.4 Storage Capacity

| Parameter | Estimate |
|---|---|
| **Volume of dried algae per ton CO2** | ~1-2 m³/t CO2 (depends on bulk density of dried/compressed biomass) |
| **Burial capacity per hectare of desert (5m deep)** | ~25,000-50,000 m³ = ~12,500-50,000 t CO2 equivalent |
| **For 1 Gt CO2/yr over 100 years** | ~100-200 km³ total volume, or ~20,000-40,000 km² at 5m depth |
| **Available hyper-arid desert globally** | ~15-20 million km² (Sahara alone: ~9 million km²) |
| **Constraint** | Burial capacity is **not** the limiting factor — available pond area for growing algae is far more constraining |

**Bottom line:** The desert has essentially unlimited burial capacity relative to any plausible algae production rate. The bottleneck is the pond infrastructure and coastal access, not burial space.

This is important because it means costs are more amenable to learning-rate reductions through economies of scale and construction optimization.

**Cost reduction pathways:**
1. **Construction scale economies:** Mass deployment of standardized pond designs reduces per-hectare construction costs
2. **Solar PV cost declines:** Desert solar electricity approaching $0.01-0.02/kWh further reduces OPEX
3. **Operational learning:** Optimized algae strain management, harvesting timing, and water recycling
4. **Unlined ponds:** If compacted desert soil provides adequate impermeability, eliminating HDPE liners dramatically reduces CAPEX
5. **Gravity-fed seawater:** Site selection to minimize or eliminate pumping energy
6. **Automation:** Remote sensing, autonomous paddlewheels, and automated harvesting reduce labor costs

### 6.4 Revenue Streams (Beyond Carbon Credits)

| Revenue Source | Potential Price | Market Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbon removal credits (voluntary market)** | $50-400/t CO2 | Growing; $2-5B/yr total voluntary market | Premium for verified, permanent removal |
| **Carbon removal credits (compliance market)** | $50-100/t CO2 (EU ETS range) | Large if algae CDR is included | Not yet included in most compliance markets |
| **US 45Q tax credit** | $180/t CO2 (for DAC with storage) | Uncapped | Unclear if land-based algae burial qualifies as "DAC" under current 45Q rules |
| **Animal feed (algae meal)** | $500-2,000/t dry biomass | Large (global animal feed: ~1.2 Bt/yr) | High protein content (~30-60%); competes with soy/fishmeal |
| **Fertilizer / soil amendment** | $100-500/t dry biomass | Large | Nutrient-rich; organic certification possible |
| **Bioplastics feedstock** | $200-1,000/t dry biomass | Growing | Algae oils and carbohydrates as bioplastic precursors |
| **Aquaculture feed** | $1,000-5,000/t dry biomass | ~$200B/yr global aquaculture market | High-value application for specific algae species |
| **Pigments (astaxanthin, beta-carotene)** | $2,000-50,000/t product | Niche but high-value | Requires specific species and extraction; may conflict with burial for CDR |

**Revenue tension:** If the algae biomass is sold as feed, fertilizer, or bioplastics, the carbon is NOT permanently sequestered — it re-enters the carbon cycle. Brilliant Planet's CDR model requires burial, meaning revenue from biomass utilization is forfeited. The business model depends on carbon credit revenue being sufficient to justify burying the biomass rather than selling it. At current voluntary credit prices ($50-400/t CO2), burial competes with biomass sale value ($100-2,000/t dry biomass, which is roughly $55-1,100/t CO2 equivalent). This tension may resolve as carbon credit prices rise or as CDR-specific credit markets mature.

- **Net CO2 removal** from atmosphere (primary purpose)

- **Desert land utilization** — converts barren land to productive use without displacing ecosystems or agriculture
- **No deforestation** — unlike some bioenergy pathways
- **Local economic development** — creates jobs in remote desert regions (Morocco, etc.)
- **Potential co-benefits** — if partial biomass is diverted to animal feed, reduces pressure on wild fish stocks and soy cultivation

### 7.8 MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification)

| MRV Aspect | Approach | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|
| **Biomass production measurement** | Weigh harvested biomass; measure water volume and algae concentration | Easy — contained system with measurable flows |
| **Carbon content analysis** | Laboratory elemental analysis of dried biomass samples (CHN analyzer) | Easy — standard analytical chemistry |
| **Net CO2 captured** | Mass balance: carbon in buried biomass minus lifecycle emissions (energy, materials, transport) | Moderate — requires comprehensive LCA |
| **Burial verification** | Physical inspection of burial sites; GPS/survey documentation; ground-penetrating radar | Easy-Moderate |
| **Permanence monitoring** | Periodic sampling of buried biomass for decomposition indicators; moisture monitoring at burial sites | Moderate — requires long-term monitoring program |
| **Additionality** | Demonstration that CO2 removal would not occur without the project | Moderate — desert algae ponds are clearly additional (would not exist naturally) |
| **Leakage accounting** | Ensure no significant emissions displacement elsewhere in the value chain | Low difficulty — self-contained system |

**MRV advantage over ocean-based CDR:** Microalgae ponds have a significant MRV advantage because the entire system is contained and measurable. Inputs (seawater, nutrients, energy) and outputs (dried biomass, brine) can be precisely quantified. This contrasts sharply with ocean-based approaches (ocean alkalinity enhancement, ocean seaweed sinking) where measurement of actual CO2 removal in the open ocean is extremely challenging.

**MRV standards:** Brilliant Planet has engaged with Puro.earth and other carbon credit registries to develop methodology for algae-based CDR credits. The contained nature of the system makes it well-suited to rigorous MRV protocols.

### 8.2 Critical Assessment

**Microalgae ponds cannot reach 10 Gt/yr as a standalone CDR method.** The fundamental constraint is suitable coastal desert land. Even using every hectare of available hyper-arid coastal desert globally (~20-45 million hectares), the maximum theoretical capture is ~1-2.25 Gt CO2/yr.

**Realistic contribution to a 10 Gt CDR portfolio:**

| Scenario | Microalgae Pond Contribution | % of 10 Gt Target | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conservative** | 100-500 Mt CO2/yr | 1-5% | 2050-2060 |

**How microalgae ponds fit in a CDR portfolio:** Microalgae ponds are best understood as one component of a diversified CDR strategy. Their strengths (low energy intensity, low cost potential, biological self-replication, seawater-only operation) complement other methods:

### 9.4 Data Notes and Caveats

**Important caveats on the data in this document:**

1. **Early-stage technology:** Brilliant Planet is at TRL 5-6 (pilot scale). Many numbers cited are projections or targets, not demonstrated commercial-scale performance. The 30-hectare pilot was still being commissioned as of early 2025.

2. **Productivity claims:** The ~30-80 t CO2/ha/yr capture rate is a company-stated range based on pilot data. Independent, peer-reviewed verification of these figures at scale is limited. Academic literature on raceway pond productivity (20-40 g DW/m²/day) is broadly consistent but was developed for biofuel/feed applications, not CDR.

3. **Cost estimates:** The $50-150/t CO2 target is a projected cost at commercial scale, not a demonstrated cost. Current pilot-scale costs are likely significantly higher. Cost projections assume optimistic learning rates and scale-up success.

4. **Permanence of desert burial:** The claim of 1,000-10,000+ year permanence is based on the analogy with organic materials preserved in arid environments (mummies, Dead Sea Scrolls, Atacama fossils). However, there is no direct long-term monitoring data for large-scale biomass burial in desert conditions. Climate change could potentially alter precipitation patterns in some desert regions over millennial timescales.

5. **Nutrient supply:** The assumption that natural seawater provides sufficient nutrients may not hold at very large scales or at non-upwelling sites. Nutrient supplementation costs and lifecycle emissions could significantly affect economics and net carbon removal.

6. **Land availability estimates:** The 200,000-450,000 km² figure for suitable coastal desert is an order-of-magnitude estimate based on global desert and coastline data. Detailed ground-truth surveys of specific sites are needed to determine actual developable area accounting for terrain, access, permitting, and other constraints.
